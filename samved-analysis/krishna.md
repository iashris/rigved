# Krishna's evolution from Upanishadic sage to supreme deity

Krishna's transformation from a cryptic Upanishadic reference to the central figure of Hindu devotion represents one of religious history's most remarkable theological syntheses. The evidence reveals not a single deity's gradual elevation but the **convergence of at least three distinct traditions**—a Vrishni tribal hero-cult, pastoral cowherd worship, and Vedic Vishnu theology—orchestrated over approximately **1,200 years** (800 BCE to 400 CE). The "Jesusified" or syncretized anti-caste thesis finds substantial support but requires significant qualification: while Krishna worship likely originated outside Brahmanical orthodoxy, its ultimate form in the Bhagavad Gita paradoxically reinforces rather than subverts caste duty (svadharma).

## The Chandogya passage establishes Krishna's antiquity

The earliest datable reference to Krishna appears in **Chandogya Upanishad 3.17.6**, composed approximately **800-600 BCE**. The passage names "Krishna Devakiputra" (Krishna, son of Devaki) as a student of the sage Ghora Angirasa, who taught him that human life itself constitutes a sacrifice. Ghora's teachings identify **tapas** (austerity), **dana** (charity), **arjava** (uprightness), **ahimsa** (non-violence), and **satyavacana** (truthfulness) as the offerings—notably the first recorded mention of ahimsa as ethical principle. The specificity of "Devakiputra" strongly suggests identification with the later mythological Krishna, though some scholars argue for interpolation or homonymous identity.

Patrick Olivelle's authoritative dating places the Chandogya among the two earliest Upanishads, pre-Buddhist in composition. The teachings bear striking parallels to Bhagavad Gita themes: the sacrifice-as-life metaphor, ethical virtues as spiritual currency, and meditative practice at death. Yet critically, **this Krishna is a student receiving wisdom, not a divine teacher imparting it**—the theological transformation to supreme deity would require another millennium.

The Rigveda (8.96.13-15) mentions a "Krishna at Amsumati" who battled Indra with ten thousand followers—a passage medieval commentator Sayana interpreted as describing a demon's defeat. Modern scholars like Benjamin Preciado-Solis see evidence of an independent Krishna tradition, possibly a tribal leader whose conflicts with Indra reflect historical Aryan-indigenous struggles. The Yamuna River identification and pastoral setting prefigure later narratives, but the interpretive controversy remains unresolved.

## Arjuna originated as an epithet of Indra

The name "Arjuna" means "white/bright/silver" in Sanskrit, creating deliberate contrast with "Krishna" (black/dark). Rigvedic usage connects "arjuna" adjectivally with Indra and his vajra weapon. The Mahabharata explicitly identifies Arjuna as Indra's divine son, inheriting his martial prowess and receiving divine weapons. Scholars Alf Hiltebeitel and Georges Dumézil demonstrated how the **Vishnu-Indra alliance** against the demon Vritra in Vedic mythology transforms into the **Krishna-Arjuna partnership** of the epic—a systematic transposition of divine mythology into heroic narrative.

The epic reveals Arjuna shares Krishna's name as an epithet; he is also called "Krishna" (dark-complexioned). Vyasa declares the Pandavas are "partial incarnations of five Indras." This doubling and mirroring suggests the Krishna-Arjuna dyad represents a theological reconstruction of older divine partnerships, with human characters serving as vehicles for cosmic relationships.

## Archaeological evidence confirms worship by 300 BCE

Material evidence establishes that organized Vasudeva worship existed centuries before our earliest complete manuscripts:

The **Heliodorus Pillar** at Besnagar (circa 113-110 BCE) provides the most dramatic testimony. A Greek ambassador named Heliodorus, serving King Antialkidas, erected a Garuda-standard column inscribed: "This Garuda-pillar of Vāsudeva, **the God of Gods**, was erected here by Heliodorus, a **Bhagavata** [devotee]." The inscription demonstrates that by 113 BCE, Vasudeva was worshipped as supreme deity (devadeva), had attracted foreign converts, employed sophisticated theological terminology, and used Garuda iconography later standard for Vishnu.

**Coins of Agathocles of Bactria** (190-180 BCE), discovered at Ai-Khanoum, Afghanistan in 1970, show Vasudeva-Krishna with conch and discus on one side, Balarama-Sankarshana with mace and plough on the other. These are the **earliest known numismatic representations of Vaishnava deities**, proving standardized iconography existed by the late second century BCE and had spread to Greco-Bactrian territories.

The **Nanaghat inscription** (1st century BCE) by Satavahana Queen Naganika invokes "Samkarshana and Vāsudeva" in that order—with the elder brother listed first, indicating Sankarshana's original priority. The **Hathibada-Ghosundi inscriptions** (2nd-1st century BCE) in Rajasthan first connect the pair with Narayana, describing a "Narayana-vatika" (sacred compound) for "Samkarshana-Vāsudeva." The **Mora Well inscription** (circa 15 CE) near Mathura mentions images of the "five Vrishnis" installed in a stone temple, identifying them collectively as "bhagavatam."

Regarding **Dwaraka**, S.R. Rao's underwater excavations (1983-1990) discovered structural remains, **120+ stone anchors**, pottery similar to Late Harappan types, and coins. His claims of 15th-century BCE dating remain contested; more recent research suggests visible structures may be historical or medieval. UNESCO lists Dwaraka as an underwater heritage site while noting "debate is still ongoing" regarding dating.

## Three streams merged into Vaishnavism

Scholarly consensus identifies three originally distinct traditions that fused:

**Stream 1: The Vrishni Hero Cult.** Vasudeva was a deified hero of the Vrishni tribe in the Mathura region. Panini's grammar (5th-4th century BCE) mentions "vasudevaka" meaning a devotee of Vasudeva, indicating established worship. Patanjali's Mahabhashya (circa 150 BCE) states Vasudeva is "the name of one who is pre-eminently worshipful, i.e., God" and contains the phrase "Vasudeva killed Kamsa"—proving the narrative was already ancient. The **five Vrishni heroes** (Panchaviras)—Sankarshana, Vasudeva, Pradyumna, Aniruddha, and Samba—became cosmic principles through the **Vyuha doctrine** of Pancharatra theology.

**Stream 2: Krishna-Gopala Tradition.** The pastoral cowherd Krishna emerged separately, likely from **Abhira tribal traditions**. The Abhiras were nomadic cattle-herding communities in the lower Indus Valley who migrated to Saurashtra by the 1st century CE. Significantly, the original Mahabharata makes **no mention of Krishna's childhood**—he appears only as an adult. The **Harivamsa** (1st-3rd century CE), appended to the epic, first develops the Vrindavan childhood narratives: birth exchange, Putana-killing, Kaliya subduing, Govardhan lifting, and rasa-lila dance. The "amorous dalliances with gopis" originated from Abhira traditions where "greater freedom of the sexes" existed.

**Stream 3: Vedic Vishnu-Narayana.** Vishnu appears in the Rigveda as a minor solar deity associated with his "three strides" across the cosmos. His importance grew through the Brahmanas. Narayana was a separate deity, possibly originating from the Himalayan Badari region. The Hathibada-Ghosundi inscriptions show Vasudeva-Sankarshana being associated with Narayana by the 1st century BCE. Full integration occurred through the Mahabharata and Bhagavad Gita, with Vasudeva-Krishna identified as Vishnu's incarnation and Narayana established as the supreme form.

**Merger timeline:**

- **4th century BCE**: Vasudeva worshipped independently among Vrishnis
- **2nd-1st century BCE**: Association with Narayana confirmed
- **1st century CE**: Mora inscription shows five Vrishnis as Bhagavatam
- **1st-3rd century CE**: Harivamsa adds childhood narratives; Gopala tradition merges
- **4th century CE**: Vishnu becomes central; full avatar doctrine develops

## Sankarshana was originally senior

Early inscriptional evidence consistently places **Sankarshana (Balarama) before Krishna-Vasudeva**, indicating the elder brother initially held cultic priority:

- Agathocles coins: Sankarshana on obverse (primary side)
- Nanaghat inscription: "Samkarshana and Vasudeva"
- Ghosundi inscription: "Samkarshana-Vasudeva"

Sankarshana's associations are agricultural: his weapons are the **plough (hala)** and **pestle (musala)**, earning epithets "Halayudha" (plough-wielder) and "Haladhara" (plough-holder). His serpent associations—identification with Shesha/Ananta, the cosmic serpent—connect to widespread **Naga worship** associated with water, fertility, and agricultural protection.

Krishna's pastoral cattle-herding imagery may have had broader appeal than Sankarshana's agricultural symbolism. The shift accelerated when the Bhagavad Gita positioned Krishna as divine teacher, and theological absorption into the Vishnu avatar system elevated Krishna while relegating Sankarshana to emanation status.

## Greek sources confirm worship circa 300 BCE

**Megasthenes**, Greek ambassador to Chandragupta Maurya (circa 300 BCE), reported in his _Indica_ that the "Sourasenoi" (Shurasenas) worshipped "Herakles" in their land, with two cities—**Methora** (Mathura) and **Kleisobora** (possibly Krishnapura)—and a navigable river, the **Jobares** (Yamuna). Greeks equated this deity with Herakles based on shared attributes: exceptional strength, monster-slaying, the club as weapon, semi-divine status, and fertility associations.

This identification may apply better to **Balarama** than Krishna—Balarama's primary weapon is the gada (club), his physical strength is emphasized, and some Mathura art shows "Herculean" depictions. The debate continues, but the crucial point remains: **organized worship at Mathura was prominent enough by 300 BCE to attract the attention of a foreign ambassador**.

Buddhist and Jain texts provide competitive appropriations. The **Ghata Jataka** (No. 454) retells Krishna's story with the Buddha's chief disciple Sariputta incarnated as Vasudeva-Krishna, subordinating the deity to Buddhist framework. Jain texts incorporate Krishna among the **63 Shalaka-purushas** (illustrious beings) but strip him of divine status—portraying him as a heroic but flawed human who, due to violence (even righteous violence), goes to **hell** after death, while the non-violent Balarama achieves liberation.

## Radha appears only in medieval literature

**Radha is entirely absent from early Sanskrit texts**—a crucial fact for understanding Krishna's theological evolution:

- **Mahabharata**: No Radha
- **Harivamsa**: No Radha
- **Vishnu Purana**: No Radha
- **Bhagavata Purana** (9th-10th century CE): Gopis appear collectively; no specific Radha named

The **Gatha Saptashati** (Gaha Sattasai), a Prakrit anthology attributed to Satavahana king Hala (dating disputed: 1st-4th century CE), may contain an early Radha reference, but the text "exists in many versions" with poems "changed over time, sometimes deleted and replaced"—making interpolation impossible to exclude.

**Jayadeva's Gita Govinda** (12th century CE, Bengal/Odisha) is definitively the **first major text treating Radha as Krishna's beloved**. Britannica confirms it as "the earliest-known poem dealing with the theme of the divine lovers, Radha and Krishna." Jayadeva "Sanskritized Radha and assured her immortality in Indian life and literature."

Why was Radha "invented"? She fulfilled theological needs Rukmini could not:

- **Parakiya love** (illicit relationship) creates greater intensity than marital love
- **Viraha bhakti** (devotion through separation) expresses the soul's longing for God
- **Democratization**: The pastoral Radha made devotion accessible to masses, unlike the royal Rukmini
- **Emotional range**: Transgressive love provided richer devotional vocabulary

The **1717 debate** at Nabak Jafara Khan's court formally decided Radha was parakiya (another's woman)—her theological function requires the scandal of illicit love.

## The Bhagavad Gita synthesizes three traditions

The Gita's dating remains contested, with most scholars placing composition between **200 BCE and 200 CE**. J.A.B. van Buitenen argued persuasively that the Gita was "conceived and created in the context of the Mahabharata"—not a "wandering text" that drifted into the epic but a work designed to resolve its central dharmic dilemma: "a war which was both just and pernicious."

The Gita achieves what Alf Hiltebeitel called "**the sealing achievement of the Hindu Synthesis**" by unifying:

**Jnana Yoga** (Upanishadic knowledge): The text accommodates both impersonal Brahman and personal deity. Krishna declares himself "the basis of the impersonal Brahman" (14.27), resolving the tension by making the impersonal derivative of the personal.

**Karma Yoga** (transformed Vedic ritual): The external fire sacrifice becomes internal. Actions performed as "yajna to the Supreme Lord" (3.9) purify without creating karmic bondage. **Nishkama karma** (action without attachment to fruits) resolves the ritual-vs-renunciation tension that had divided Indian thought. The Yajnavalkyan karma doctrine is transformed: one need not renounce action, only attachment to results.

**Bhakti Yoga** (personal devotion): Complete surrender to Krishna as Bhagavan offers the most accessible path. Chapter 12 confirms bhakti as "both the easiest and the highest path to salvation."

Chapter 11's **Vishvarupa darshana** (cosmic form vision) reveals Krishna as containing all universes, past-present-future, all beings—achieving the theological synthesis of a personal God who is simultaneously the impersonal Absolute.

## The anthropological thesis requires qualification

The evidence substantially supports viewing Krishna as emerging from **non-Vedic traditions** later absorbed into Brahmanical Hinduism:

**Supporting evidence:**

- "Krishna" literally means "black/dark"—early Tamil Sangam literature describes **Mayon**, a dark pastoral god
- The flute association is absent from Vedic deities and appears first in 1st-2nd century CE art
- Multiple Krishna figures merged: warrior-sage Vasudeva + pastoral Gopala
- Abhira tribal connections to cowherd traditions
- Early texts show Brahmanical "hesitancy to accept a non-Aryan tribal deity"
- Parallel absorption pattern evident in **Jagannath cult** (Puri), where tribal elements persist

**D.D. Kosambi** argued Krishna "had risen from the pre-Aryan people" and his Rigvedic enmity to Indra "reflects the historical struggle of dark pre-Aryans against marauding Aryans." **Suvira Jaiswal** contends Brahmins "seized upon the devotional cult of Vasudeva-Krishna... to infuse Brahmanical social ethics into this popular cult."

**Critical qualification:** The Gita **does not subvert caste**—it reinforces it. Krishna explicitly endorses svadharma: "It is far better to perform one's own duties, even though faultily, than another's duties perfectly" (3.35). The text responds to Buddhist/Jain heterodoxy by reasserting Brahmanical duty-ethics. Romila Thapar argues the Gita's "urgency had to do with the current debate on ahimsa provoked by Buddhist and Jaina teaching."

The "Jesusified" analogy captures the synthesis of diverse traditions into a coherent theological figure but misses the political dimension: unlike early Christianity's social radicalism, Krishna-theology as codified in the Gita upholds hierarchical order. The absorption process **co-opted heterodox devotional energy while channeling it into orthodox social structures**.

## Conclusion

Krishna's emergence represents a paradigmatic case of religious synthesis. The evidence reveals not simple absorption of a tribal deity but a complex **multi-century convergence** of warrior-hero cult, pastoral folk religion, and Vedic theology—mediated by political alliances, economic exchange along trade routes, and theological competition with Buddhism and Jainism.

The thesis of Krishna as "syncretized anti-caste figure" captures real historical dynamics but requires refinement. Early Bhagavata worship likely offered egalitarian alternatives to Brahmanical ritualism. Yet the tradition's codification in the Bhagavad Gita redirected this energy toward svadharma—caste duty performed without attachment—rather than caste abolition. The synthesis absorbed heterodox devotional intensity while preserving orthodox social architecture.

What emerged was neither purely Aryan nor non-Aryan, neither wholly orthodox nor heterodox, but something genuinely new: a personal deity accessible through emotional devotion, philosophically sophisticated enough to address Upanishadic metaphysics, and socially conservative enough to secure Brahmanical endorsement. This triple achievement explains Krishna's eventual dominance over Sankarshana, his absorption of Vishnu-Narayana theology, and his lasting centrality to Hindu religious life. The flute-playing cowherd and the cosmic Vishvarupa are not contradictions but complementary faces of a deity constructed precisely to resolve such tensions—a theological synthesis whose very incoherence as history becomes its coherence as mythology.
